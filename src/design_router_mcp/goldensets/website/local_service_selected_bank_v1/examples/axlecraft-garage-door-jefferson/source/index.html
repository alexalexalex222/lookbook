Technical Briefing

Straight Answers.

The questions our dispatch line hears most, answered the way a technician would answer them.

My spring snapped. Can I still open the door?

Do not try. A snapped torsion spring means nothing is counter-balancing the full weight of the door. Disconnect the opener so it does not strain the motor, leave the door where it sits, and call the dispatch line. Spring work is the one job we never recommend attempting yourself.

Should I repair or replace my door?

One failed component on a structurally sound door is a repair. Multiple bent panels, rusted tracks, or a door approaching two decades of service usually make replacement the cheaper call over five years. When it is close, we quote both and show you the math.

What are high-cycle springs?

Springs are rated in open-close cycles. Builder-grade springs are typically rated around 10,000 cycles; the high-cycle variants we stock are rated for 25,000 or more. For a modest difference in hardware cost, a busy household roughly triples the service life of the loudest failure point on the system.

Do you work on all opener brands and drive types?

Yes. Chain, belt, screw, and wall-mount jackshaft drives are all standard work, regardless of brand. Our trucks carry universal logic boards, gear kits, safety sensors, and remotes, so most opener faults are fixed in a single visit.

Why is my door suddenly loud?

Grinding usually means worn rollers or a track edging out of alignment. A sharp pop under load points to spring fatigue. A general rattle is loose hardware. All three are cheap to correct at the tune-up stage and expensive to ignore — noise is the system asking for service before it fails.
