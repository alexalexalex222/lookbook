No. 02 The idea

A stopped watch is right twice a day. Nadir is built to never be stopped, or wrong.

Every choice inside Nadir — the free-sprung balance, the mass of the case, the geometry of the lever — exists to hold a rate, not to announce one. There is no complication here that isn't the hour. No date wheel jumping at midnight to prove the machine is alive.

What's left is a small, exact discipline: a watch that agrees with itself, day after day, whether or not anyone is checking. We check anyway. That's the job.

None of that is visible from across a room, which is the point. The next section is the only part of Nadir that is — the case, the crown, the hands — and every one of those surfaces was decided by the same discipline, not by what photographs well.

No. 03 The object

Nothing on this case is for looking at.

Every surface you can see is doing mechanical work, or protecting something that is. Turn it in the light and read it — five decisions you can feel with a thumbnail before you ever wind it.

01 — Case

Machined, then made quiet.

Cut from a single billet of grade-5 titanium and coated matte black, the case is bead-blasted against the light rather than polished toward it. A watch that catches attention on a wrist is a watch that stopped thinking about the wrist.

02 — Movement

The part you're not meant to watch move.

A manually wound calibre, finished and adjusted by one person, visible only through the case back if you choose to turn it over. We don't build a show movement. We build one that runs.

03 — Escapement

Where the accuracy actually lives.

A free-sprung balance, regulated without a fast/slow lever to knock out of true. Poise, not adjustment, is what holds the rate — the same reason a wheel spins truer than it's pushed.

04 — Dial & hands

Legible before it's beautiful.

Applied indices, cut and polished by hand, angled to catch light at arm's length in the exact condition you actually check a watch in: a glance, not a study.

05 — Strap & clasp

The part that touches you, and only that.

A quick-release strap you can swap without tools, and a milled clasp that adjusts on the wrist — not just in the box.

No. 04 The motion

The part you can hear is the least of it.

A movement doesn't fail loudly. It drifts — a half-second here, a degree of amplitude there — long before you'd ever notice by ear. The real work is in the parts you can't hear: an escapement adjusted so the same swing happens whether the mainspring is fully wound or nearly spent.

Isochronism is the promise that today's second is the same length as yesterday's — nothing more, nothing less.

No. 05 The build

Five stages. One person carries the watch through all of them.

Nothing here is outsourced to a line. A single build runs start to finish before the next one begins.

  1. 01

    Billet

    A single block of grade-5 titanium is cut for the case — not cast, not stamped. What's removed matters as much as what's left.

  2. 02

    Movement

    The calibre is assembled dry, oiled by hand, and run until it settles into its own rate.

  3. 03

    Regulation

    One watchmaker adjusts the same unit across positions — dial up, dial down, crown left — until the rate agrees with itself everywhere.

  4. 04

    Casing

    Movement, dial, and hands are cased under filtered air, then the case is pressure-tested with the crown sealed.

  5. 05

    Certification

    The finished watch is timed a final time, on its own, and the person who regulated it signs the card that ships inside the case.
