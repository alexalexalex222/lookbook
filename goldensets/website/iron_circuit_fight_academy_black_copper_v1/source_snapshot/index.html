The Iron Circuit standard

Why the mat feels different here

Three things every member notices in their first week — before they notice anything else.

Beginner-first curriculum

Every track opens with a fundamentals on-ramp. Nobody is placed into live rounds before they know the movement.

Coached intensity, not chaos

Every round has a coach watching pace and control. Effort is expected; recklessness is corrected on sight.

Consent-based partner work

Tap early, ask before drilling something new, and any partner can pause a round. No exceptions, no ego.

01 Programs

Five tracks. One progression.

Pick where you want to start. Most members blend tracks once the fundamentals click — MMA Fundamentals is where Striking and Grappling meet.

Fundamentals

The on-ramp for people who have never trained. Low-pressure sessions that teach movement, terminology, and gym etiquette before you join the main floor.

  • No experience or fitness baseline assumed
  • Learn the warm-up, language, and safety rules
  • Clear path into Striking, Grappling, or MMA next

Typical session shape

  1. Mat orientation
  2. Guided warm-up
  3. Movement drilling
  4. Etiquette & Q&A
  5. Cool-down
Format
Small group, 45 min
Contact
None to light
Gear
Just water & athletic wear

Most members spend one to two weeks here before choosing a track.

Striking

Boxing and Muay Thai footwork, guard, and combinations drilled on pads and bags, building toward controlled partner work and light technical sparring.

  • Stance, distance, and defensive structure first
  • Pad rounds, bag work, and clinch entries
  • Optional controlled sparring once fundamentals are solid

Typical round structure

  1. Shadow warm-up
  2. Technique breakdown
  3. Pad rounds
  4. Bag work
  5. Mobility cool-down
Format
Group rounds, 60 min
Contact
Scaled to your level
Gear
Wraps & gloves (loaners available)

Muay Thai clinch entries are introduced once boxing footwork is consistent.

Grappling

Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu and wrestling fundamentals — positional control, escapes, and submission chains taught through live positional rounds at a safe pace.

  • Positional hierarchy, escapes, and base
  • Gi and no-gi options as you progress
  • Live rounds scaled to experience and consent

Typical round structure

  1. Movement drilling
  2. Technique breakdown
  3. Positional sparring
  4. Live rounds (optional)
  5. Cool-down
Format
Group rounds, 75 min
Contact
Controlled, position-led
Gear
Rashguard or gi (ask at desk)

New members start in positional rounds only — live rolling comes later, by choice.

MMA Fundamentals

Where the tracks connect. Takedown entries, floor control, and transitions between striking range and the ground, built for members who already have some time on the mat.

  • Entry requires baseline time in Striking or Grappling first
  • Takedown entries, sprawls, and striking-to-grappling transitions
  • Round structure mirrors real bout pacing, at training intensity

  5. Cool-down

03 First Visit

Your first weeks, mapped

Nobody gets thrown into sparring on day one. Here is how the first month actually goes.

  1. 1

    Before you arrive

    Book & brief

    Reserve a trial session below. We confirm your slot and tell you exactly what to bring — usually just water and athletic clothes.

  2. 2

    Day one

    Floor tour & warm-up

    A coach walks you through the mat zones, etiquette, and the warm-up so nothing feels unfamiliar before training starts.

  3. 3

    First sessions

    Fundamentals, no pressure

    You drill core movements at your own pace. Partner work is light and supervised — consent and control come first, always.

  4. 4

    End of week one

    Pick your track

    A coach helps you choose Striking, Grappling, or both, and sets a simple weekly plan that fits your schedule and goals.

  5. 5

    Weeks two through four

    Build the rhythm

    You settle into your track, start recognizing faces on the mat, and a coach checks your pacing. This is where training turns into a habit.

05 The Floor

Four zones, one mat

The training floor is laid out in defined zones so striking, grappling, and conditioning never collide mid-round.

Zone 01

Striking Bay

Bags, pads, and a taped ring line for boxing and Muay Thai rounds. Mirrors along the back wall for solo shadow work.

Zone 02

Grappling Mats

Sprung matting sized for positional rounds and live rolling, with a clear engagement circle for BJJ and wrestling.

Zone 03

Conditioning Corner

Sleds, ropes, and bodyweight stations for warm-ups and the conditioning work built into every class.

Zone 04

Recovery & Cubbies

Secure cubbies, a changing area, and space to stretch out after class before you head home.

08 Code of the Mat

Intensity without ego

Every member trains under the same rules, from the first trial session onward. This is what keeps the floor safe at any intensity.

  1. Bow in, bow out

    Acknowledge the mat and your coach at the start and end of every session. It sets the tone before anything physical happens.

  2. Hygiene first

    Clean training gear, trimmed nails, no open cuts on the mat. Respect for your training partners starts here.

  3. Tap early, tap often

    Tapping is not losing. It is how everyone in the room gets to train again tomorrow.

  4. Ask before you drill something new

    Found a submission or strike you haven't drilled with this partner before? Ask first, every time.

  5. No ego rounds

    Sparring is training, not a fight. If a coach calls a round down, it is down — no arguments.

  6. Coach has final say

    On pace, on pairings, on when someone sits a round out. The coach's call is the call, every time.

  7. New faces get room

    Training with someone new or a first-week beginner? Dial your intensity back, not theirs.

  8. Leave the floor how you found it

    Wipe down, rack the pads, reset the zone. The next class starts on a clean mat.
